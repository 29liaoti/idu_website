Life Before the Bomb

Hiroshima, Japan — Pre-1945

Chapter One

A City Once Alive

Before August 6, 1945, Hiroshima was one of Japan's most vibrant and historically significant cities — a place of culture, commerce, and community. Founded in 1589, the city had grown into a major regional hub with a population of approximately 350,000 people.

Hiroshima was a significant military headquarters for the Japanese Imperial Army, and its geography — spread across six river deltas flowing into the Seto Inland Sea — made it both beautiful and strategically important. Families lived in wooden homes in close-knit neighbourhoods, children attended school, and markets bustled with daily life.

By the early 1940s, Japan was deep in the Pacific War. Resources were rationed, young men were conscripted, and the civilian population endured mounting hardships. Yet Hiroshima, unlike other major Japanese cities, had been largely spared from the American firebombing campaigns — a fact that would make it the target of something entirely new.

Many residents had no idea a weapon capable of destroying an entire city even existed. On the night of August 5, 1945, Hiroshima went to sleep not knowing it had only hours left.

📍 The City

Hiroshima was the 7th-largest city in Japan, spread across the Ōta River delta, with thriving markets, schools, temples, and hospitals.

👥 Population

Approximately 350,000 civilians and military personnel called Hiroshima home on the morning of August 6, 1945.

⚔️ Wartime Context

Japan had been at war since 1937. Civilians faced food rationing, factory mobilisation, and widespread anxiety as Allied forces advanced across the Pacific.

🏫 Mobilised Civilians

Schools had been converted for war production. Thousands of students were mobilised as labourers — many were in the open city centre the morning the bomb fell.

🎯 Why Hiroshima?

US planners chose Hiroshima partly because it had not been heavily bombed, ensuring the effect of the atomic bomb could be clearly measured. Its flat terrain and dense urban core made it an ideal target.

02

The Moment of the Bombing

August 6, 1945 — 8:15 AM — Hiroshima, Japan

Chapter Two

8:15 AM.
Everything Changed.

On the morning of August 6, 1945, the American B-29 bomber Enola Gay dropped "Little Boy" — the world's first atomic bomb used in warfare — over the city of Hiroshima. The bomb detonated approximately 600 metres above the city. Within seconds, the temperature at ground zero reached several thousand degrees Celsius.

8:15 AM — Detonation

The Flash

A blinding flash of light, brighter than the sun, illuminated the sky. People within 1 kilometre had no warning. Many were vaporised instantly, leaving only shadows burned into stone walls — permanent silhouettes of human beings caught in the moment.

8:15 AM — Seconds Later

The Shockwave

A massive pressure wave flattened buildings across 13 square kilometres. The shockwave traveled faster than sound — structures collapsed before anyone could hear the blast coming. Flying debris became deadly projectiles across the entire city.

8:15 AM — Minutes Later

The Firestorm

Hundreds of individual fires ignited by the thermal pulse merged into a single catastrophic firestorm. Temperatures were so extreme that roof tiles melted, and everything within a 2km radius was reduced to ash and rubble.

Within Hours

Black Rain

Radioactive particles and soot rose into the atmosphere and fell back to earth as "black rain" — dark, oily droplets that contaminated water sources and the skin of survivors far beyond the epicentre.

Days to Weeks

Radiation Sickness

Survivors who appeared physically unharmed began dying. Vomiting, hair loss, bleeding, and immune system collapse — acute radiation syndrome — claimed thousands who had believed they had survived.

"I thought my younger sister and I were the only ones left alive in the world."
— Hibakusha survivor, age 13 at the time of the bombing
03

The Immediate Effects

The human cost in the hours, days, and weeks that followed

Chapter Three

The Human
Cost

The bomb did not discriminate. Civilians, children, hospital workers — all were caught in the blast. The Hibakusha, meaning "explosion-affected people," were left to survive in a city that no longer existed, with injuries medicine had never encountered before.

🔥

Thermal Burns

Intense heat caused severe burns across exposed skin. Survivors close to the blast had their clothes burned away. Many walked with their skin hanging from their arms and hands, seeking help that could barely be found.

💨

Blast Injuries

The shockwave caused massive internal injuries from sudden pressure change. Flying glass, tiles, and debris penetrated bodies across the entire city in fractions of a second.

☢️

Acute Radiation Sickness

Survivors who seemed unharmed began dying days later from radiation poisoning — vomiting, hair loss, and internal bleeding as their immune systems collapsed entirely.

🏚️

The Destroyed City

70% of Hiroshima's buildings were completely destroyed. Hospitals, schools, and infrastructure collapsed, leaving survivors with no medical care and no clean water.

👧

Children Like Sadako

Children who survived the blast developed leukemia years later. Sadako Sasaki, only 2 years old at the time, developed leukemia at 12. While folding 1,000 origami cranes, she became a global symbol of peace and nuclear abolition.

🩺

Overwhelmed Medicine

Of Hiroshima's 298 doctors, 270 were killed or injured. The remaining medical staff had no supplies and had never encountered radiation injuries before — they were completely unprepared.

04

The Aftermath & Outcome

What followed — and how the world changed forever

Chapter Four

After the Smoke
Cleared

Three days after Hiroshima, a second atomic bomb was dropped on Nagasaki on August 9, 1945. Japan surrendered on August 15, ending World War II. But the consequences of Hiroshima did not end with the war — they rippled outward into history, politics, science, and human suffering for generations.

🕊️

Japan's Surrender

Emperor Hirohito announced Japan's unconditional surrender on August 15, 1945 — ending WWII in the Pacific. The atomic bombings were a decisive, if deeply controversial, factor in this decision.

🌐

The Nuclear Arms Race

Hiroshima triggered a Cold War nuclear arms race between the United States and the Soviet Union. By the 1980s, the world had accumulated over 60,000 nuclear warheads — enough to destroy civilisation many times over.

⚖️

International Law & Treaties

The bombings accelerated the creation of international humanitarian law. The Geneva Conventions were updated in 1949, and landmark treaties like the Non-Proliferation Treaty (1968) and the UN Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (2021) followed.

🏛️

Hiroshima Peace City

In 1949, Japan designated Hiroshima an official "Peace Memorial City." The Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum and the Genbaku Dome — the preserved ruins of the explosion's epicentre — became symbols of the world's commitment to "Never Again."

📣

Hibakusha Testimony

Survivors — the Hibakusha — dedicated their lives to sharing their testimonies around the world. Their stories formed the moral backbone of the global anti-nuclear movement and continue to be the most powerful argument against nuclear weapons.

🔬

Medical & Scientific Legacy

Studying the health effects on Hiroshima survivors fundamentally advanced the world's understanding of radiation, cancer, and genetics — research that continues to shape medicine today.

05

Segregation of the Hibakusha

The survivors who were made to feel like the guilty ones

Chapter Five

Survivors — Yet
Treated as Outcasts

Surviving the bomb was only the beginning of the Hibakusha's suffering. In the years and decades that followed, the approximately 650,000 registered atomic bomb survivors faced a second form of violence — social discrimination, stigma, and systematic exclusion from Japanese society, rooted in fear, misunderstanding, and prejudice.

💍

Marriage Discrimination

Hibakusha were widely considered "undesirable" marriage partners. Families feared their children or grandchildren would inherit radiation-related illness. Many survivors were forced to hide their status or faced outright rejection from potential partners and their families.

💼

Employment Exclusion

Employers discriminated against Hibakusha, fearing they were ill, contagious, or would become a financial burden through sick leave. Many survivors concealed their exposure — even from doctors — to avoid losing their livelihoods.

🧬

Fear of Contamination

A widespread, scientifically unfounded belief held that radiation could be "caught" like an infection. Hibakusha were shunned in public, avoided on public transit, and excluded from communities — even by those who had also survived the bombing.

🏥

Medical Neglect

For years after the bomb, Hibakusha struggled to receive proper medical care. The Japanese and US governments were initially slow to provide support. Survivors had to fight for official recognition and medical assistance that acknowledged their unique health needs.

🤐

Silenced by Shame

Many Hibakusha chose not to identify themselves publicly for decades, fearing the social consequences. Their silence meant that the world was slow to understand the full human cost of the atomic bombing — and that their suffering went unacknowledged for far too long.

🌏

Korean Hibakusha

An estimated 40,000–70,000 Koreans — brought to Hiroshima as forced labourers — were among the victims. Korean Hibakusha faced double discrimination: stigmatised both as atomic bomb survivors and as a colonial minority in Japan, with little access to reparations or recognition for decades.

A Timeline of Recognition

1945
Atomic Bomb Casualty Commission (ABCC) established by the US to study survivors — but it provided no medical treatment, only data collection. Survivors felt used as scientific subjects.
1957
Japan passes the Atomic Bomb Survivors Relief Law, providing some medical funding — though eligibility criteria were narrow and many survivors remained excluded.
1978
ABCC is reorganised into the Radiation Effects Research Foundation (RERF), a joint US–Japan body providing ongoing health studies of survivors.
2009
After decades of legal battles, Korean Hibakusha living abroad gain access to Japanese government medical benefits following a Supreme Court ruling.
2016
President Barack Obama becomes the first sitting US president to visit Hiroshima — meeting with Hibakusha survivors in a historic act of acknowledgment, though no formal apology was issued.
Today
Fewer than 100,000 registered Hibakusha remain alive. Their average age exceeds 85. Their testimonies are being urgently recorded and preserved before the last living witnesses are gone.
"We Hibakusha carry the weight of the bomb in our bodies every day. We speak so that it never happens again."
— Hibakusha survivor testimony, Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum

The Science Behind the Bomb

How nuclear fission unleashes the energy held in atoms

Chapter Six

Splitting
the Atom

The atomic bomb works through nuclear fission — the splitting of heavy atomic nuclei to release an enormous amount of energy. At its core is Einstein's famous equation, E=mc², which tells us that even a tiny amount of mass can be converted into a staggering amount of energy, because the speed of light squared is an incomprehensibly large number.

The bomb dropped on Hiroshima, "Little Boy," used Uranium-235. The bomb dropped on Nagasaki, "Fat Man," used Plutonium-239. Both exploited the same fundamental physics — a chain reaction of splitting atoms — but through different designs.

Understanding this science is not just academic. It is precisely because this energy is so powerful that its military application is so devastating — and why the same physics, when used constructively, has the potential to power entire nations cleanly and safely.

🔬 How Fission Works — "Little Boy" (U-235)

The Chain Reaction — Step by Step

1
A Free Neutron Strikes a U-235 Nucleus

A single neutron is fired at an unstable Uranium-235 atom. The nucleus absorbs it and becomes violently unstable — Uranium-236 — for a fraction of a second.

2
Fission — The Nucleus Splits

The nucleus splits into two smaller atoms (Barium and Krypton) releasing a massive amount of energy and, critically, 2–3 additional free neutrons.

3
Chain Reaction Begins

Each of those neutrons strikes another U-235 atom, releasing more energy and more neutrons. The reaction doubles with every generation — escalating exponentially.

4
Critical Mass is Reached

When enough fissile material is assembled (the "critical mass"), the chain reaction becomes self-sustaining — and then explosive. In "Little Boy," 64kg of U-235 was used.

5
E = mc² Becomes Reality

A tiny fraction of the uranium's mass is converted into pure energy. Even a 1% mass conversion of 64kg releases energy equivalent to ~13,000 tonnes of TNT — destroying an entire city.

Key Insight: The same fission physics that powers a bomb is used in nuclear reactors — but in a controlled way. In a reactor, control rods absorb excess neutrons, slowing the chain reaction to a steady, manageable pace that generates heat for electricity instead of an explosion.

Nuclear Technology for Good

The same science, directed toward humanity

Chapter Seven

Two Outcomes.
One Science.

The very physics that destroyed Hiroshima has also saved millions of lives — and may yet be one of our most powerful tools in addressing climate change, cancer, and global energy poverty. The technology is neutral. It is the choices we make with it that define its legacy.

Nuclear Technology as a Healer

The Science
That Saves

When directed toward human welfare, nuclear science has become one of the most powerful tools in modern medicine, clean energy, and scientific discovery. Today it saves millions of lives every year.

⚕️   Radiation therapy for cancer treatment
🧠   Nuclear imaging: PET & SPECT scans
⚡   Clean nuclear energy powering cities
🧪   Radioisotopes in diagnostics & research
🚀   Nuclear-powered deep space exploration

Nuclear Technology as a Weapon

The Science
That Destroys

On August 6th, 1945, the most advanced technology of the era was dropped on Hiroshima. In a single moment, everything humanity had built was turned against itself — with consequences still felt today.

Technological Insights

Nuclear Science
in Service of Life

Nuclear technology, when governed by ethical frameworks and directed toward peaceful purposes, represents one of the most versatile and promising tools available to humanity. Here are some of its most impactful positive applications today.

🩺

Cancer Treatment

Radiation therapy uses precisely targeted beams of radiation to destroy cancer cells while protecting surrounding tissue. It is a first-line treatment for many cancer types, saving millions of lives annually. Proton beam therapy — a nuclear technique — offers even greater precision.

🧠

Medical Imaging

PET (Positron Emission Tomography) and SPECT scans use radioactive tracers to image metabolic activity inside the body in real time. These techniques detect cancers, neurological disorders, and heart disease earlier and more accurately than conventional methods.

⚡

Clean Nuclear Energy

Nuclear power plants produce electricity with near-zero carbon emissions. A single nuclear plant can power millions of homes. As the world transitions away from fossil fuels, next-generation reactors — including small modular reactors (SMRs) — offer a safer, cleaner path.

🌾

Food Safety & Agriculture

Radiation is used to sterilise food, extending shelf life and killing harmful pathogens without chemicals or preservatives. Nuclear techniques also help breed more resilient, higher-yield crop varieties — reducing hunger in food-insecure regions.

🚀

Space Exploration

Nuclear radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) have powered spacecraft like Voyager, Cassini, and Curiosity — enabling missions to the outer planets and beyond where solar panels cannot function. Future deep space missions will rely on nuclear propulsion.

💧

Water & Environmental Science

Nuclear techniques are used to trace water sources, detect groundwater contamination, and study environmental pollution. Isotope analysis helps scientists understand climate change, glacial movements, and ocean circulation patterns.

"Science is a powerful instrument. How it is used — whether it is a blessing or a curse to mankind — depends on mankind and not on the instrument."
— Albert Einstein, 1946

Technology is neither good
nor evil — we are.

Hiroshima teaches us that the greatest scientific discoveries carry the heaviest moral weight. The same atom that powers a hospital scanner once levelled a city. The same physics that could help us defeat cancer and climate change was first used to destroy 140,000 lives.

As long as we remember the Hibakusha, we carry the responsibility they gave us: to choose wisely, to govern carefully, and to build a world where such a weapon is never used again.

Nuclear weapons must never be used or built again.

The Moment of the Bombing

August 6, 1945 — 8:15 AM — Hiroshima, Japan

Chapter One

8:15 AM.
Everything Changed.

On the morning of August 6, 1945, the American B-29 bomber Enola Gay dropped "Little Boy" — the world's first atomic bomb used in warfare — over the city of Hiroshima. The bomb detonated approximately 600 meters above the city. Within seconds, the temperature at ground zero reached several thousand degrees.

8:15 AM — Detonation

The Flash

A blinding flash of light brighter than the sun illuminated the sky. People within 1 kilometre had little warning. Many were vaporized instantly, leaving only shadows burned into stone walls.

8:15 AM — Seconds Later

The Shockwave

A massive pressure wave flattened buildings across 13 square kilometres. The shockwave traveled faster than sound — buildings collapsed before anyone could hear the blast coming.

8:15 AM — Minutes Later

The Firestorm

Fires ignited across the city merged into a single firestorm. The heat was so intense it melted roof tiles and burned everything within a 2km radius to the ground.

Within Hours

Black Rain

Radioactive debris rose into the atmosphere and fell back as "black rain" — oily, dark droplets that contaminated water sources and skin of survivors far from the epicentre.

The Immediate Effects

The human cost in the hours, days, and weeks that followed

Chapter Two

The Human
Cost

The bomb did not discriminate. Civilians, children, hospital workers — all were caught in the blast. The Hibakusha, meaning "explosion-affected people," were left to survive in a city that no longer existed, with injuries medicine had never encountered before.

🔥

Thermal Burns

The intense heat caused severe burns across exposed skin. Survivors close to the blast had their clothes burned away. Many walked with their skin hanging from their arms and hands.

💨

Blast Injuries

The shockwave caused internal injuries from the sudden change in pressure. Flying debris became deadly projectiles that penetrated bodies across the city.

☢️

Acute Radiation Sickness

Survivors who seemed physically unharmed began dying days later from radiation poisoning — vomiting, hair loss, and internal bleeding as their immune systems collapsed.

🏚️

The Destroyed City

70% of Hiroshima's buildings were completely destroyed. Hospitals, schools, and infrastructure collapsed, leaving survivors with no medical help and no clean water.

👧

Children Like Sadako

Children who survived the blast developed leukemia and other cancers years later. Sadako Sasaki, who was 2 years old at the time, developed leukemia at age 12. Her story became a symbol of peace.

🩺

Overwhelmed Medicine

Of Hiroshima's 298 doctors, 270 were killed or injured. The remaining medical staff had no supplies, no guidance, and had never encountered radiation injuries before.

The Lasting Scars

The consequences that echoed through generations

Chapter Three

A Wound That
Never Fully Healed

The atomic bombing of Hiroshima did not end on August 6th, 1945. Its effects rippled across decades — in the bodies of survivors, in the grief of families, and in the nuclear arms race it triggered between world powers.

80,000+ Killed instantly
140,000 Dead by end of 1945
350,000+ Total Hibakusha registered
12,500+ Nuclear warheads still exist today
"
"We Hibakusha carry the weight of the bomb in our bodies every day. We speak so that it never happens again."
— Hibakusha survivor testimony, Hiroshima Peace Memorial Museum

Technology is neither good
nor evil — we are.

As long as people continue to create nuclear weapons, humanity remains in danger of destroying itself with the very tools it built to improve the world. The Hibakusha spoke so we would remember. We remember so it never happens again.

Nuclear weapons must never be used or built again.
